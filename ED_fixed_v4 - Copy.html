Start with speed-torque characteristics — these are asked every year and also appear as part of M2 braking questions. Master the equation \( J\frac{d\omega}{dt} = T_e - T_L \) and its implications.
  • Draw all four speed-torque curves: constant torque, fan-type (\(T_L \propto \omega^2\)), constant power, and combined loads.
  • Four-quadrant operation is conceptual but marks-heavy — practice drawing the quadrant diagram with correct labeling of motoring/braking directions.
  • Stability: understand graphical criterion — stable equilibrium point where motor characteristic intersects load curve with negative slope difference.
🔹 B. Key Equations to Know

Fundamental equation of motion for motor-load system:

\[ J\frac{d\omega_m}{dt} = T_e - T_L \]

Where \(J\) = moment of inertia (kg·m²), \(\omega_m\) = mechanical angular velocity (rad/s), \(T_e\) = electromagnetic torque, \(T_L\) = load torque.

Equivalent inertia referred to motor shaft (with gear ratio \(a = N_1/N_2\)):

\[ J_{eq} = J_m + \frac{J_L}{a^2} \]

Fan-type load torque:

\[ T_L = K\omega^2 \]

Condition for stability at equilibrium point (speed \(\omega_0\)):

\[ \frac{dT_e}{d\omega}\bigg|_{\omega_0} < \frac{dT_L}{d\omega}\bigg|_{\omega_0} \]
🔹 C. Common Mistakes
  • Confusing the direction of torques in four-quadrant operation — always define positive direction first.
  • Forgetting to label which quadrant is "Forward Motoring", "Forward Braking", "Reverse Motoring", "Reverse Braking."
  • Not converting between rpm and rad/s (\(\omega = 2\pi N/60\)) in numerical problems.
  • Stability analysis — don't just state "stable/unstable"; draw the intersection point and show slope signs.

Energy relations during starting is a derivation-type question asked in all 4 years. Memorize the derivation completely. The key result \(W_{loss} = \frac{1}{2}J\omega_0^2\) (energy stored in rotor = energy wasted in resistance during no-load starting) must be known cold.
🔹 B. Key Equations & Derivations

Energy stored in rotating mass:

\[ W_{KE} = \frac{1}{2}J\omega_0^2 \]

Energy supplied by motor during starting (no-load, linear accel.):

\[ W_{supplied} = J\omega_0^2 \]

Energy dissipated in resistance:

\[ W_{loss} = W_{supplied} - W_{KE} = \frac{1}{2}J\omega_0^2 \]

Therefore: Energy dissipated in starting resistors = Energy stored in rotating parts (independent of resistance value for no-load starting)

Energy loss in rotor resistance during starting of induction motor:

\[ W_R = s \cdot W_{input} \]

Where \(s\) = slip at which motor reaches steady state.

Plugging energy dissipation (for DC motor):

\[ W_{plug} = \frac{3}{2}J\omega_0^2 \]

Dynamic braking energy (dissipated in braking resistance \(R_B\)):

\[ W_{dyn} = \frac{1}{2}J\omega_0^2 \]

Condition for multi-step starting (minimum energy loss):

\[ \frac{R_1}{R_2} = \frac{R_2}{R_3} = \cdots = \text{constant ratio} = \sqrt[n]{\frac{R_{total}}{R_{last}}} \]

Heating & cooling curves + classes of duty appear in 3/4 years. Draw the heating curve equation and understand what thermal time constant means physically. Classes S1–S6 definitions are pure memory work — prepare a compact table.
🔹 B. Key Equations

Motor temperature rise during heating:

\[ \theta(t) = \theta_{max}\left(1 - e^{-t/\tau_{th}}\right) \]

Motor temperature during cooling:

\[ \theta(t) = \theta_1 \cdot e^{-t/\tau_{cool}} \]

Where \(\tau_{th}\) = thermal time constant = \(\frac{mS}{A}\) (mass × specific heat ÷ cooling coefficient)

Final/maximum temperature rise:

\[ \theta_{max} = \frac{P_{loss}}{A} \]

Where \(A\) = heat dissipation coefficient, \(P_{loss}\) = losses in motor

RMS rating for variable duty cycle:

\[ P_{rms} = \sqrt{\frac{P_1^2 t_1 + P_2^2 t_2 + \cdots}{t_1 + t_2 + \cdots}} \]

Flywheel sizing for load equalization:

\[ GD^2 = \frac{375 \cdot \Delta P \cdot t}{\Delta N} \]

The dynamic model (transfer function) is the backbone of this module. Practice deriving it from scratch using KVL on armature circuit and mechanical equation. The block diagram with inner current loop and outer speed loop must be drawn correctly in every exam answer.
🔹 B. Key Equations & Transfer Functions

DC Motor Armature Circuit (KVL):

\[ V_a = E_b + I_a R_a + L_a \frac{dI_a}{dt} \]

Back-EMF: \( E_b = K_\phi \omega_m \)

Mechanical equation:

\[ T_e - T_L = J\frac{d\omega_m}{dt} + B\omega_m \]

Electromagnetic torque: \( T_e = K_\phi I_a \)

Transfer function — Armature current to voltage (neglecting back-EMF for inner loop):

\[ G_a(s) = \frac{I_a(s)}{V_a(s)} = \frac{1/R_a}{1 + s\tau_a} \quad \text{where } \tau_a = \frac{L_a}{R_a} \]

Transfer function — Speed to torque (mechanical subsystem):

\[ G_m(s) = \frac{\Omega_m(s)}{T_e(s)} = \frac{1/B}{1 + s\tau_m} \quad \text{where } \tau_m = \frac{J}{B} \]

Overall open-loop plant transfer function:

\[ G_p(s) = \frac{\Omega_m(s)}{V_a(s)} = \frac{K_m}{(1+sT_e)(1+sT_m)} \]

Where \(T_e = L_a/R_a\) (electrical time constant), \(T_m = J/B\) (mechanical time constant), \(K_m = K_\phi/(R_a B)\)

Chopper modeled as gain with delay:

\[ G_{ch}(s) = \frac{V_{dc}}{V_{c,max}} \cdot \frac{e^{-sT_s/2}}{1} \approx \frac{K_{ch}}{1 + s T_d} \]

Where \(T_d = T_s/2\) (half the switching period)

🔹 D. Controller Design Summary
The cascade control structure has two loops: inner (fast) current loop and outer (slow) speed loop. Design inner loop first.
  • Current Controller (inner loop): PI controller typically used. Bandwidth 5–10× greater than speed loop. Gain chosen to achieve desired closed-loop current time constant (≈ \(T_a/4\) to \(T_a/10\)).
  • Speed Controller (outer loop): PI controller. Reference is speed set-point. Output is current reference for inner loop. Integral action eliminates steady-state speed error.
  • Anti-windup: Current limiter at output of speed controller to protect motor from overcurrent.

🔹 B. Key Concepts — V/f Control

Air gap flux in induction motor:

\[ \Phi \propto \frac{V_s}{f} \]

To keep flux constant (and hence torque capability), maintain \(V/f = \text{constant}\) as frequency is varied.

Synchronous speed:

\[ N_s = \frac{120f}{P} \text{ rpm} \quad \Leftrightarrow \quad \omega_s = \frac{4\pi f}{P} \text{ rad/s} \]

Slip speed remains approximately constant for same torque at different supply frequencies (constant flux control):

\[ \omega_{slip} = \omega_s - \omega_r \approx \text{constant at given } T_L \]

At very low frequency: voltage drop across stator resistance is significant → voltage boost is applied to maintain flux:

\[ V_{boost}(f) = V_{rated} \cdot \frac{f}{f_{rated}} + \Delta V \]

🔹 C. Self-Controlled vs True Synchronous Mode
  • True Synchronous Mode: Stator frequency is fixed externally (from supply). Motor must synchronize — risk of losing synchronism at large disturbances. No rotor position feedback.
  • Self-Controlled Mode: Stator frequency is derived from rotor position (via shaft encoder or back-EMF sensing). Motor CANNOT lose synchronism because frequency always tracks rotor position. Used in variable-speed synchronous motor drives.
✅ Self-controlled mode essentially converts synchronous motor into a DC motor behavior — the commutation is done electronically based on rotor position instead of mechanically (brushes/commutator).

2025 Q5(b) A 3kW, 400V, 50Hz, 4-pole, 1400 rpm, delta connected induction motor has the following parameters referred to the stator: R₁ = 2.5Ω, R₂ = 4.5Ω; X₁ = X₂ = 6Ω. 2025 Q6(a) Enumerate the similarities between a BLDC motor and a self-controlled synchronous motor drive.

M3 2025 7 marks Hard Repeated 2023
2025 Q6(b) Derive the power rating of an electric motor with continuous duty with variable load by the method of equivalent torque.
Key formula: \(T_{eq} = \sqrt{\frac{\sum T_i^2 t_i}{\sum t_i}}\). Copper losses \(\propto I^2 \propto T^2\). Same heating for variable load as constant load \(T_{eq}\) over same time. Derive by equating heat generated. Motor should be rated for \(T_{eq}\). Also derive equivalent current method similarly.
M2 2025 5 marks Medium Repeated 2024
2025 Q6(c) Derive the expression for energy loss during starting of a DC shunt motor?
Key result: \(W_{loss} = \frac{1}{2}J\omega_0^2\) — always equal to KE stored, regardless of starting resistance. See 2024 Q2(a). Key result: Energy from supply = 2 × KE stored. Half is always wasted. More steps → less current fluctuation but same total energy loss.
